Question
Let's see why these inequalities hold true. $$ P(A) + P (B) − 1 \le P (A \cap B) $$ . For two events A and B in the same sample space, The intersection is at most equal to max(P(A), P(B)) and at least max(0, |P(A)-P(B)|-1). Therefore, It is not possible for P(A) + P(B) -1 to be greater than \(P(A\cap B)\). If P(A) = 1 and P(B) = 1, then this becomes an equality $$ P (A \cap B) \le P (A \cup B) $$ This one is more straight forward because the probability of two events happening at the same time can never be more than either event happening. Once again, when P(A) and P(B) both equal 1, these are the same. Also if the both equal 0 $$ P (A \cup B) \le P (A) + P (B) $$ This is true because inclusion-exclusion principle. When we do P(A)+P(B), we are counting the intersection of the two events twice. Whenever A and B are disjoint, this will be an equality.
